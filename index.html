Introduction

Medicinal plants are valuable natural resources rich in bioactive compounds that play a vital role in the development of novel drugs. Due to this, extensive research has been carried out worldwide to study the antioxidant, antimicrobial, wound-healing, anti-ulcer, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties of plants.

The metabolite profile of endophytes is strongly influenced by the unique chemical environment of the host plant. Among endophytes, fungal endophytes are considered promising sources of biologically active compounds with significant medicinal importance.

Aloe vera is a well-known medicinal plant possessing antioxidant, anticancer, anti-ulcer, wound-healing, and skin-protective properties. Pharmacologically important compounds are mainly present in the parenchymatous tissue of the fleshy leaves.

Endophytes are microorganisms, including fungi and bacteria, that reside within plant tissues and form a symbiotic relationship with their host in highly competitive environments. Aloe vera is one of the oldest medicinal plants and is known as the “healing plant” due to the presence of acemannan, which exhibits immunomodulatory, antitumor, antibacterial, and antifungal activities.

Aloe barbadensis (syn. Aloe vera), commonly known as Indian Aloe or Barbados Aloe, is a perennial herb with fleshy leaves of high medicinal value. The name Aloe is derived from the Arabic word “Alloeh” meaning “shining bitter substance,” while “vera” in Latin means “true.” Among more than 325 species of Aloe, Aloe barbadensis is the most widely cultivated due to its rapid propagation and high therapeutic value.

Aloe vera plants are susceptible to several fungal and bacterial diseases. Fungal pathogens are more commonly associated with Aloe vera and cause diseases such as leaf spot, collar rot, and root rot, which adversely affect plant growth, development, and medicinal quality. Some fungal pathogens produce mycotoxins that pose serious risks to human and animal health.

Review of Literature

Endophytic fungi isolated from desert plants are among the least explored organisms with significant medicinal potential. Fuad Ameen et al. (2021) isolated an endophytic fungus from Aloe vera collected from the Asir desert, Saudi Arabia, and identified it as Preussia africana using ITS sequencing.

Kamalakannan et al. (2008) reported the first occurrence of leaf spot disease caused by Alternaria alternata on Aloe barbadensis in India. Severe disease incidence was observed in Erode and Madurai districts of Tamil Nadu. The pathogen produced grey colonies with olive green margins on PDA, and characteristic obclavate conidia.

Mohammed Ali Abdul Razzaq Al-Nuaimy et al. (2024) isolated 53 endophytic fungi from Aloe vera leaves and reported a colonization rate of 44.16%. Chemical analysis using HPLC and GC-MS revealed aloin, aloe-emodin, fatty acids, and several bioactive compounds.

Divya Singh et al. (2024) isolated fungal pathogens responsible for Aloe vera leaf spot disease and identified Alternaria alternata, Nigrospora oryzae, and Curvularia lunata as major pathogenic species. Maximum disease severity was observed for Alternaria alternata.

Aim and Objectives

Aim

To isolate and identify fungal pathogens responsible for diseases in Aloe vera plants in order to understand their diversity and develop effective disease management strategies.

Objectives

  1. To collect healthy and infected Aloe vera plant samples.
  2. To isolate fungal pathogens using microbiological techniques.
  3. To identify fungal isolates based on morphological characteristics.
  4. To study pathogenicity on healthy Aloe vera plants.
  5. To document diversity and prevalence of fungal pathogens.

Plan of Work

  1. Literature review
  2. Sample collection
  3. Isolation and culturing of fungi
  4. Pure culture preparation
  5. Morphological identification
  6. Pathogenicity tests
  7. Data analysis and reporting

Materials and Methods

Materials

  • Infected and healthy Aloe vera plant samples
  • Autoclave, incubator, microscope, centrifuge
  • Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA), Sabouraud Dextrose Agar (SDA)
  • 70% ethanol, sodium hypochlorite, sterile distilled water
  • Petri plates, inoculation needles, micropipettes, gloves

Methods

Plant samples were surface sterilized, cut into small segments, and inoculated on PDA plates. Plates were incubated at 25–28°C for 5–7 days. Fungal colonies were subcultured to obtain pure isolates. Identification was based on colony morphology and microscopic examination. Pathogenicity was confirmed using Koch’s postulates.

Conclusion

Aloe vera is an important medicinal plant with high demand in Indian and international markets. Fungal diseases such as black spot significantly reduce quality, yield, and market value. Chemical fungicides, although effective, pose environmental and health risks. Therefore, eco-friendly disease management strategies involving biocontrol agents and plant extracts are recommended for sustainable Aloe vera cultivation.
