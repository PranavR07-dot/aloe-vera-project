Introduction

Plants are natural resources of beneficial compounds that can help in the development of novel drugs. Therefore, the world has extensively studied the antioxidant, antimicrobial, wound healing, anti-ulcer, anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties of plants. The metabolite profiles of endophytes are greatly influenced by the unique chemical environment of the host plant. Especially fungal endophytes are hopeful sources for novel biologically active compounds with great impact on medicine.

Aloe vera is a medicinal plant with antioxidant, anticancer, anti-ulcer, wound healing properties, and skin protection effects. The pharmacologically important compounds are found in the parenchymatous tissue of the fleshy parts of the plant. This plant was collected from highly stressed regions.

Endophytes are microorganisms in the form of fungi and bacteria that exist within various tissues of the host plants and form a symbiotic relationship with their host in a highly competitive environment.

Aloe vera is one of the oldest medicinal plants and is known as the “healing plant” due to the presence of acemannan in its leaves, which possesses immunomodulatory, antitumor, antibacterial and antifungal activities (Ameen et al., 2021).

Aloe barbadensis (syn. Aloe vera), popularly known as Indian Aloe or Barbados Aloe, is a perennial herb with fleshy leaves that have great medicinal value. The name Aloe is derived from the Arabic word “Alloeh” meaning “shining bitter substance” while “vera” in Latin means “true”.

According to Adodo (2009), over 325 species of the genus Aloe have been identified. Some common species include Aloe barbadensis Miller, A. saponaria, A. chinensis, A. variegata, A. ferox, A. latifolia and A. curacao. Among these, Aloe barbadensis is more popular worldwide due to faster propagation and higher therapeutic value and is therefore referred to as the “True Aloe”.

Aloe plants are attacked by a number of fungal and bacterial pathogens that cause diseases such as leaf spots, collar rot and root rot. These diseases affect growth, development and therapeutic potential of the plant. Fungal contamination also poses serious risks to human health due to the production of mycotoxins (Mina Kumari et al., 2020).

Review of Literature

Fuad Ameen et al. (2021) studied endophytic fungi isolated from Aloe vera collected from the Asir desert region of Saudi Arabia. Molecular identification using ITS1, ITS4 and 5.8S rRNA sequencing revealed the fungus as Preussia africana, belonging to the family Sporormiaceae. This study highlighted the potential of desert endophytes as sources of bioactive compounds.

Kamalakannan et al. (2008) reported severe leaf spot disease of Aloe vera in Coimbatore, Erode and Madurai districts of Tamil Nadu. Black sporulation appeared in the centre of leaf spots. The pathogen was isolated on Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA) and identified as Alternaria alternata based on morphological characteristics.

Mohammed Ali Abdul Razzaq Al Nuaimy et al. (2024) isolated 53 endophytic fungi from 120 Aloe vera leaf segments with a colonization rate of 44.16%. Chemical analysis revealed the presence of aloin, aloe-emodin, fatty acids and multiple bioactive compounds using HPLC and GC-MS techniques.

Divya Singh et al. (2024) isolated five fungal species from Aloe vera leaf spot disease including Alternaria alternata, Nigrospora oryzae, Curvularia lunata, Aspergillus niger and Penicillium species. Alternaria alternata showed maximum disease severity (49.05%).

Aim and Objectives

Aim

To isolate and identify the fungal pathogens responsible for diseases in Aloe vera plants in order to understand their diversity and develop effective disease management strategies.

Objectives

  1. To collect healthy and infected Aloe vera plant samples.
  2. To isolate fungal pathogens using microbiological techniques.
  3. To identify fungal pathogens based on morphological characteristics.
  4. To study pathogenicity on healthy Aloe vera plants.
  5. To document diversity and prevalence of fungal pathogens.

Plan of Work

  1. Literature review on fungal diseases of Aloe vera.
  2. Collection of infected plant samples.
  3. Surface sterilization and isolation on PDA.
  4. Subculturing to obtain pure fungal cultures.
  5. Microscopic and morphological identification.
  6. Pathogenicity testing using Koch’s postulates.
  7. Data analysis and documentation.
  8. Preparation of report and conclusion.

Materials and Methods

Materials

  • Infected Aloe vera plant samples
  • Autoclave, incubator, microscope, centrifuge
  • Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA), SDA
  • 70% ethanol, sodium hypochlorite
  • Petri plates, micropipettes, gloves

Methods

Plant samples were surface sterilized using ethanol and sodium hypochlorite. Tissue segments were inoculated on PDA and incubated at 25–28°C for 5–7 days. Emerging colonies were subcultured to obtain pure isolates. Identification was carried out using morphological and microscopic characteristics.

Summary

The isolation and identification of fungal pathogens from Aloe vera involve systematic collection of diseased plant samples showing symptoms such as leaf spots, blight, and necrotic lesions. The infected leaves are thoroughly washed, surface sterilized, and cut into small segments before being cultured on Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA) under controlled laboratory conditions.

The fungal growth obtained is purified using single hyphal tip or sub-culturing techniques to ensure isolation of pure cultures. Identification of fungal pathogens is carried out by preparing microscopic slides and observing morphological and cultural characteristics such as colony color, texture, spore shape, size, and arrangement using a compound microscope.

Common fungal pathogens isolated from Aloe vera include Alternaria alternata, Fusarium oxysporum, Fusarium solani, Nigrospora oryzae, Cladosporium herbarum, Stemphylium botryosum, Aspergillus niger, and Penicillium species.

Pathogenicity tests are conducted by inoculating healthy Aloe vera leaves with fungal spores to confirm disease causation by re-isolating the pathogen, following Koch’s postulates. Among the isolated fungi, Alternaria alternata is frequently identified as the major pathogen responsible for leaf spot disease, exhibiting high disease incidence and severity.

Other pathogenic fungi such as Nigrospora oryzae and Curvularia lunata also show significant pathogenicity on Aloe vera leaves. The identification process integrates morphological analysis, cultural behavior on PDA media, and supportive molecular identification techniques such as NCBI database comparison.

Conclusion

Aloe vera is an important medicinal plant with high demand in both the Indian and international markets. However, black spot disease has emerged as a serious problem for Aloe vera growers across the country. This disease significantly reduces the quality, yield, and market value of Aloe vera plants, making its effective management essential.

Since Aloe vera is extensively used in medicinal and cosmetic industries, contamination by fungal pathogens can pose serious risks to human health. Several fungal pathogens are known to produce mycotoxins in infected plant tissues and on growth substrates, which may be harmful to both humans and animals when contaminated plant material is used.

Although chemical fungicides provide quick and effective control of plant diseases, their continuous application is associated with several drawbacks. These include the development of pathogen resistance, disease resurgence, harmful effects on non-target organisms, environmental pollution, and potential risks to human health. Additionally, pesticide residues may remain in treated plant materials and negatively affect the pharmacologically important constituents of Aloe vera.

Due to these limitations, there is a growing need to develop safer and eco-friendly alternatives for disease management. The use of biocontrol agents and plant-based extracts for controlling Colletotrichum gloeosporioides and other fungal pathogens offers a promising solution, as these methods are cost-effective, environmentally safe, and pose minimal risk to users.

Therefore, an integrated disease management approach that combines compatible biocontrol agents, plant-derived products, and eco-friendly chemicals can be effectively exploited in the future for sustainable management of black spot disease in Aloe vera. Such strategies will help maintain plant health, preserve medicinal quality, and ensure environmental safety.
