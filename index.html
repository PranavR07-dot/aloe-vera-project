Introduction

Plants are natural resources rich in beneficial bioactive compounds that play an important role in the development of novel drugs. Extensive research has been carried out worldwide to study the antioxidant, antimicrobial, wound-healing, anti-ulcer, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic properties of medicinal plants.

The metabolite profile of endophytes is strongly influenced by the unique chemical environment of the host plant. Among these, fungal endophytes are considered promising sources of biologically active compounds with significant medicinal importance.

Aloe vera is a well-known medicinal plant possessing antioxidant, anticancer, anti-ulcer, wound-healing, and skin-protective properties. Pharmacologically important compounds are mainly present in the parenchymatous tissue of the fleshy leaves.

Aloe vera (Aloe barbadensis Mill.) is one of the oldest medicinal plants in human history and is often referred to as the “healing plant” due to the presence of acemannan, a compound with immunomodulatory, antitumor, antibacterial, and antifungal properties.

Despite its medicinal importance, Aloe vera plants are susceptible to several fungal and bacterial diseases. Fungal pathogens cause leaf spot, collar rot, and root rot, adversely affecting plant growth, yield, and therapeutic quality. Since Aloe vera is widely used in medicinal and cosmetic products, fungal contamination poses a serious risk to human health due to the production of harmful mycotoxins.

Review of Literature

Fuad Ameen et al. (2021) isolated and identified endophytic fungi from Aloe vera plants collected from desert regions of Saudi Arabia. Molecular identification using ITS sequencing revealed Preussia africana as a potential endophyte with significant bioactive properties.

Kamalakannan et al. (2008) reported Alternaria alternata as the causal agent of leaf spot disease in Aloe vera in Tamil Nadu, India. The pathogen was isolated on Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA) and identified based on colony morphology, conidia shape, and size.

Mohammed Ali Abdul Razzaq Al-Nuaimy et al. (2024) isolated 53 endophytic fungi from Aloe vera leaves, including Aspergillus terreus, Aspergillus niger, Penicillium chermesinum, and Paecilomyces variotii. Chemical analysis revealed aloin, aloe-emodin, and various fatty acids.

Divya Singh et al. (2024) identified fungal pathogens responsible for leaf spot disease of Aloe vera, including Alternaria alternata, Nigrospora oryzae, Curvularia lunata, Aspergillus niger, and Penicillium species. Among these, Alternaria alternata showed the highest disease severity.

Aim and Objectives

Aim

To isolate and identify fungal pathogens responsible for diseases in Aloe vera plants in order to understand their diversity and develop effective disease management strategies.

Objectives

  • To collect healthy and infected Aloe vera plant samples showing disease symptoms.
  • To isolate fungal pathogens from infected plant tissues.
  • To identify fungal isolates based on morphological and cultural characteristics.
  • To study the pathogenicity of isolated fungi on healthy Aloe vera plants.
  • To document the diversity and prevalence of fungal pathogens affecting Aloe vera.

Plan of Work

  1. Review of existing literature related to fungal diseases of Aloe vera.
  2. Collection of infected Aloe vera samples from different locations.
  3. Surface sterilization and isolation of fungal pathogens on PDA media.
  4. Preparation of pure fungal cultures by sub-culturing.
  5. Morphological identification using microscopic examination.
  6. Pathogenicity testing following Koch’s postulates.
  7. Data analysis and documentation of results.
  8. Preparation of report and conclusion.

Materials and Methods

Materials

  • Infected Aloe vera plant samples
  • Laboratory equipment: Autoclave, incubator, microscope
  • Culture media: Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA)
  • Chemicals: 70% ethanol, sodium hypochlorite, sterile distilled water
  • Consumables: Petri plates, inoculation needles, slides, gloves

Methods

Infected Aloe vera leaves were washed and surface sterilized using ethanol and sodium hypochlorite. Small tissue segments were inoculated on PDA plates and incubated at 25–28°C for 5–7 days.

Emerging fungal colonies were sub-cultured to obtain pure isolates. Morphological identification was performed by observing colony characteristics and microscopic features.

Pathogenicity tests were conducted by inoculating healthy Aloe vera plants and re-isolating the pathogen to confirm disease causation.

Summary

Fungal pathogens associated with Aloe vera were isolated and identified using standard microbiological techniques. Common pathogens included Alternaria alternata, Fusarium oxysporum, Nigrospora oryzae, Curvularia lunata, Aspergillus niger, and Penicillium species. Pathogenicity tests confirmed their disease-causing ability.

Conclusion

Aloe vera is an important medicinal plant with high commercial value. Fungal diseases significantly reduce plant quality and yield. Chemical fungicides pose environmental and health risks; therefore, eco-friendly disease management strategies such as biocontrol agents and plant-based extracts are recommended for sustainable cultivation of Aloe vera.
